On socializing gender roles

"While television is often a main form to pass time by at a lot of homes, readings books is a heavily used medium, even in today’s culture. In nursery settings, schools, and libraries, hourly sessions are made dedicated just for reading books to children. Children’s books, usually containing illustrations or picture medias of some sorts, ranges from using human characters to animal characters. Janice McCabe et al. investigates the aspect of gender in these books. They revealed that throughout these books, males are evidently more visible through titles and as main characters. And although disparities of character appearances have gotten better, for animal characters, the gap is still big – using pink and stereotypically female ideas to portray a girl animal character, and not a huge number of appearances either. There’s an obvious over representation of stereotype, like the use of pink, ribbons, and frill to illustrate girls or female animals. And on the other side of the spectrum, there’s a lack of appearances of girls as main characters and in titles, which contributes to the under representation. Hand in hand, these over and under representation in the wrong places (stereotypes playing huge roles) adds to the effect on what being a girl means, according to the norms being socialized to young girls.

A well-known example of portrayal which defines femininity in children media is the Disney princess figures, which has existed throughout generations for quite a while now. Since 1973, when the first Disney princess was introduced on television in the form of Snow White & the Seven Drawfs – even today, these characters are still iconic and new movies still being produced. Up to this era, media consumption of Disney princesses beyond movies, in the form of toys, is still occurring. A study done by Sarah Coyne et al. reveals that higher engagement with Disney princesses are associated with higher levels of ‘feminine’ gender-stereotypical behavior in girls. Examples of these include speaking softly, nicely, and being less dominating or smart (although there are some Disney princesses that are definitive of that norm like Mulan as an example of that, but overall the majority is otherwise.) To clarify, this does not mean that the normative idea of what’s viewed as a “girly girl” necessarily means a bad thing. But to put into consideration, on the other side of the spectrum, it also does mean that there is a very narrowed definition of femininity outside of that boxed idea of the usual..."
